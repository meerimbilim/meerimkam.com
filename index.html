Customer intelligence as a commercial advantage — surfacing what the market actually needs.
Ran a structured customer intelligence programme — 45+ discovery interviews using the Jobs-to-Be-Done framework — that surfaced adoption barriers invisible to the product and engineering teams. The findings shaped GTM positioning, informed the product roadmap, and directly resulted in a new feature being shipped. The commercial motion and the product motion became one.

Effective GTM strategy depends on knowing what the market actually wants — not what the product team assumes it wants. At Lessi AI, before any positioning or sales collateral was developed, I designed and ran a structured customer intelligence programme: 45+ discovery interviews using the Jobs-to-Be-Done framework, conducted with users across different roles, contexts, and adoption stages.

The findings were operationally significant. Adoption barriers that appeared to be product gaps turned out to be onboarding failures. Features prioritised by the roadmap were not driving user behaviour. One specific insight — surfaced through the interview process — went directly to engineering and shipped as a new product feature. The research bridged the gap between what customers experienced and what the company believed they experienced.

Acting as the cross-functional bridge between customers, product, and engineering, I translated ongoing feedback into concrete roadmap priorities and built the enablement infrastructure — onboarding content, training materials, use-case documentation — that drove activation and reduced time-to-value. Co-authored a white paper on AI adoption shared externally with enterprise partners and institutional stakeholders.

Harvard iLab
Tutelligence
Founder — Harvard Innovation Labs
Institutional partnerships at the highest level — ministry, policy, and national deployment.
Led GTM strategy and institutional partnerships for Tutelligence, an AI platform targeting government and ministry-level buyers across multiple countries. Built a pipeline of institutional demand through structured stakeholder engagement and adoption workshops — securing a signed letter of intent from a Deputy Chairman of Cabinet committing national financial backing and policy alignment.

Tutelligence, founded at Harvard iLab, targeted one of the most complex buyer segments in any market: national governments. Government procurement is characterised by long cycles, multi-stakeholder approval structures, and a requirement for demonstrated institutional credibility before any commercial conversation can begin.

The approach was to establish institutional trust before pursuing commercial outcomes. I designed and ran adoption workshops with 50+ participants — educators, institutional administrators, and ministry staff — generating 10+ letters of interest from schools and government bodies. These served as validated demand signals that created the conditions for ministry-level engagement.

The outcome: a signed letter of intent from the Deputy Chairman of the Cabinet of Ministers of a Central Asian national government — committing government financial backing, a dedicated Presidential Administration coordination team, and national policy alignment for deployment across the national curriculum. Secured prior to product launch. A demonstration of what institutional GTM looks like at the highest level of government.
